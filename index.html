The premise

You won your seat. Now what?

WestminsterSim drops you onto the back benches as a newly elected MP and hands you the decisions that build — or end — a career: which causes to champion, which colleagues to cultivate, when to rebel, and when to keep your head down. Every choice nudges your standing with the party, the press, and the voters back home.

What you'll do

A whole career, one decision at a time

Climb the greasy pole one rung at a time — catch the whips' eye, earn a junior brief, survive a reshuffle, and fight your way into the Cabinet.

Climb the greasy pole
From the green benches to the despatch box: minister, Secretary of State, Deputy PM, Prime Minister, or Leader of the Opposition.
Hundreds of decisions
Budgets, PMQs, party conference, scandals, rebellions, defections and leadership contests — each with consequences.
A real election engine
First-past-the-post seats, swing, coalitions and confidence-and-supply deals turn the national mood into a result.
Four starting eras
Begin in 2015, 2017, 2019 or 2024 — each with its own parliament, its own rivals, and its own pressures.
Rivals, allies and a record
Named front-benchers, a living cabinet, and a career timeline that remembers every office you've held.
Shape the party
Build your own agenda, win the arguments that matter to you, and remake the party in your image.

See it in motion

Every choice has a reckoning

Real party names set the scene; the people are invented. The drama is entirely yours to win or lose.

Election night

A full seat-by-seat model turns the national mood into a result — landslides, hung parliaments and knife-edge majorities, called constituency by constituency.

Leadership contests

Declare your candidacy, count the numbers in the tea room, and fight a multi-stage ballot against named rivals for the keys to the party.

The door of Number 10

Win the leadership, win the election, and walk through the famous black door — or spend a decade leading the Opposition, waiting for your moment.

Redraw the map

Form a coalition, prop up a minority, or watch a surging challenger party take the balance of power — then live with the consequences.
